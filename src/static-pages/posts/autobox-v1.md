# AUTOBOX v1

:::article-block project-scope
ComplexScan AUTOBOX v1 was a substantial hardware and software R&D project for museum-grade photogrammetric 3D scanning.

:::article-block heritage-mission
I developed the equipment and capture technology for the cultural-heritage platform now operating as OBJET.art. The work targeted digitization of art objects and cultural artifacts with enough fidelity for preservation, restoration analysis, and exhibition: detailed geometry, accurate color, shadow-free textures, and a workflow that could scale beyond a single lab setup.

:::article-block capture-line
In parallel with contributing to the partner platform, I developed an original capture technology line: a practical combination of photogrammetry, repeatable motion, light control, cross-polarization, source-data discipline, and 3D processing workflow aimed at geometry, color, and texture quality that was at the upper competitive level of its time.

:::article-block capture-technology
I worked on the capture technology line by researching photogrammetry versus laser scanning, validating RealityCapture and related tools, designing repeatable multi-angle capture, and building custom modular lighting hardware with wireless control, active cooling, sensors, and cross-polarization support. The equipment and capture configurations were specialized for difficult size ranges, from very small artifacts to large museum objects.

:::article-block fabrication
The equipment followed the same hands-on small-batch model as my other hardware lines: I assembled prototypes and early units myself, coordinated specialist contractors for fabricated parts such as laser-cut metal and acrylic, and handled final assembly, testing, and preparation for field use.

:::article-block museum-fieldwork
The prototype progressed from lab testing to operational museum scanning. I took part in planning and running on-site scanning sessions with complex equipment logistics inside museums and personally scanned the Japanese netsuke collection at the State Hermitage Museum during test production. The same technology line was also used for Benin bronze objects at the Kunstkamera.

:::article-block netsuke-collaboration
The netsuke validation was part of the OBJET.art / ArtClub Digital Heritage work for the Hermitage Under a Magnifying Glass project. Ten Japanese netsuke objects were selected with input from Anna Savelyeva, Curator of Japanese Art at the State Hermitage Museum, and British netsuke expert Max Rutherston.

:::article-block material-challenges
The objects created a strong production test: tiny carved details, himotoshi cord holes, wood, ivory, horn, glossy surfaces, translucent inserts, and complex relief that forced the capture pipeline to solve geometry, texture, color, and material-specific problems at the same time.

:::article-block visualization
Beyond capture, I also produced the artistic 3D visualization of the digitized artifacts: I visualized all the Hermitage netsuke and all the Kunstkamera bronzes, and personally scanned and visualized pieces such as a gilded Buddha ([example](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iNqxRJgrqM8)) and a Mughal jeweled dagger. These visualizations were used in videos prepared for the State Hermitage project. The collaborative results are published on the [OBJET.art / ArtClub Digital Heritage channel](https://www.youtube.com/@ArtClub_DigitalHeritage); the netsuke were captured on-site at the Hermitage ([process](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=us3vQHuTYPw)).

:::article-block production-scale
Beyond the hardware itself, I helped organize and scale the production process around 3D object processing: retouching, retopology, color calibration, large source datasets, field photography, and handoff between photographers and 3D specialists. I participated in recruiting 3D photographers, retouchers, and visualizers and in creating training materials for scanning and scan processing so the workflow could scale beyond one operator.

:::article-block platform-product
I also took part in recruiting programmers for the product side: a web 3D player and a platform for hosting and publishing cultural-heritage objects and private collections. That product line now operates as [OBJET.art](https://objet.art/) — a cultural-heritage platform and meeting point for museums, galleries, private collections, scholars, collectors, and the public.
